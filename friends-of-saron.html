About the project

A small Welsh cemetery with a big story.

Saron Cemetery sits on the hillside above Troedyrhiw in the Merthyr valley. Established in the early nineteenth century, it became the resting place of miners, ministers, shopkeepers, midwives and children from across the industrial communities of South Wales.

The Friends of Saron group formed in 2009 when local residents became concerned about the deteriorating condition of the site. Since then volunteers have cleared vegetation, repaired boundary walls, stabilised headstones and begun the careful task of recording each burial.

History

From chapel ground to community project.

1822

1822

Early nonconformist witness

Baptist worship begins in the Troedyrhiw area, with meetings held in cottages and on the mountainside long before a chapel building exists.

1835

1835

Land purchased for Saron

Land is purchased from Sir Josiah John Guest of the Dowlais Iron Company for the building of Saron Chapel and the associated burial ground.

1843

1843

Chapel and graveyard expand

Saron Chapel is extended to accommodate a growing congregation drawn from the rapidly expanding mining and ironworking community.

1900s

Late 19th – early 20th century

A valley community in stone

Burials reflect the social history of the valley: industrial accidents, epidemics, migration, revival movements and the impact of war are all recorded in the inscriptions.

1960s

1960s–1980s

Decline and closure

With the decline of heavy industry and changes in patterns of worship, chapel attendance falls. The burial ground gradually becomes overgrown and many stones begin to fail.

2009

2009

Friends of Saron formed

Concerned local residents form the Friends of Saron group to take action. Regular work days begin, and links are made with Merthyr Tydfil County Borough Council and heritage organisations.

Today

Today

Recording and restoration

Volunteers continue the painstaking work of vegetation management, stone conservation and inscription recording. The online Burials Register allows families and researchers around the world to explore Saron’s story.

Hands-on conservation

Regular work days see volunteers clearing vegetation, making safe leaning stones, repairing boundaries and improving access paths. All work is done with hand tools and great care to avoid damage.

No specialist skills are required – just appropriate clothing and a willingness to help. Training, tools and plenty of tea are provided.

Inscription & archive research

Every legible inscription is transcribed, photographed and cross-referenced with census records, parish registers, nonconformist burial records and Welsh newspaper archives.

This work feeds directly into the public Burials Register — a permanent, searchable record for families tracing their South Wales ancestry.

Community & education

We hold regular open days, guided walks and talks in partnership with local schools, Merthyr Tydfil Borough Council and heritage organisations across South Wales.

Volunteers of all ages and backgrounds are welcome — no experience required. Just bring enthusiasm for Welsh history and comfortable footwear.

Common questions

Use our Burials Register page — search by surname, first name, year of death or plot number. You can also browse row by row using the interactive plot map. If your ancestor isn't yet listed, contact us and we'll check our offline records.

Yes, Saron Cemetery in Troedyrhiw is open to visitors. We recommend wearing sturdy footwear as the ground is uneven in places. Guided tours can be arranged by contacting us in advance.
