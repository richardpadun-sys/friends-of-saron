About the project

A small Welsh graveyard with a big story.

Saron graveyard sits on the hillside above Troedyrhiw in the Merthyr valley. Established in the early nineteenth century, it became the final resting place of ironworkers, ministers, tradespeople, housewives, children and others from this part of industrial South Wales.

The Friends of Saron group formed in 2009 when local residents became concerned about the deteriorating condition of the site. Since then volunteers have cleared vegetation, repaired boundary walls, stabilised headstones and begun the careful task of recording each burial.

History

From chapel ground to community project.

1820

1820

Early days of the Independent cause in Troedyrhiw

A number of people in the area decided to start a Sunday School with help from Bethesda Chapel in Merthyr. Initially the school and services took place from house to house but later at the Old Mill and the Harp Inn.

1835

1835

Land purchased for Saron

Land is provided by Sir Josiah John Guest of the Dowlais Iron Company, who claimed ownership, for the building of Saron Chapel and the associated burial ground.

1852

1852

Chapel and graveyard expand

Saron Chapel is rebuilt to accommodate a growing congregation drawn from the rapidly expanding mining and ironworking community. The actual landowner was found to be Wyndham Lewis and he granted a lease for this purpose.

1900s

Late 19th – early 20th century

A valley community in stone

Burials reflect the social history of the valley: industrial accidents, epidemics, migration, revival movements and the impact of war are all recorded in the inscriptions.

1960s

1960s – early 2000s

Decline, closure and dereliction

With the decline of heavy industry and changes in patterns of worship, chapel attendance falls. Following the closure of the chapel in 1983 and its subsequent demolition, the burial ground progressively becomes overgrown and the condition of the memorials and boundary walls deteriorates.

2009

2009

Friends of Saron formed

Concerned local residents form the Friends of Saron group to take action. Regular work days begin, and links are made with Merthyr Tydfil County Borough Council, the landowner, the leaseholder and a variety of organisations and individuals potentially capable of providing guidance and support.

Today

Today

Recording and restoration

Volunteers continue the painstaking work of vegetation management, stone conservation and inscription recording. The online Burials Register allows families and researchers around the world to explore Saron’s story.

What we do

Practical care, research and community.

Hands-on conservation

Regular work days see volunteers clearing vegetation, making safe leaning stones, repairing boundaries and improving access paths. All work is done with hand tools and taking great care to avoid damage.

No specialist skills are required – just appropriate clothing and a willingness to help. Training, tools and plenty of tea are provided.

Inscription & archive research

Every legible inscription is transcribed, photographed and cross-referenced with census records, parish registers, nonconformist burial records and Welsh newspaper archives.

This work feeds directly into the public Burials Register — a permanent, searchable record for families tracing their South Wales ancestry.

Community & education

We hold regular open days, guided walks and talks in partnership with local schools, Merthyr Tydfil Borough Council and heritage organisations across South Wales.

Volunteers of all ages and backgrounds are welcome — no experience required. Just bring enthusiasm for Welsh history and comfortable footwear.

Wildlife & wildflower garden

A section of the graveyard has been designated as a managed wildflower habitat, supporting pollinators and providing a peaceful green space for the community.
